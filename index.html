12.Bourgeois Life – Bürgerliches Leben

The decades after the foundation of the “German Reich” in 1871 were dominated by the middle class both in the town and the countryside. After the death of the Welf duke William in 1884, a council of regency took over official duties in the Duchy of Brunswick. Although local politics gained in significance, the parties incorporated it into their platforms only incrementally. At first civic associations represented specific professional and demographic groups. In both politics and clubs and associations, middle class citizens who were successful in trade and business were in charge. The years of rapid industrial expansion and increasing civic influence in the late 19th century known as the Gründerzeit (literally, founders' period) brought brisk building activity to Wolfenbüttel. On some of the razed fortifications, stately bourgeois houses, a new building for the “Grosse Schule” high school and the Lessing Theatre were erected. New districts that emerged at the time were named Harztorwall, Lessingstrasse and Neuer Weg. The former ducal seat had finally recovered from the economic shock of the relocation of the royal court, and, at around the turn of the 20th century, developed into a wealthy bourgeois town with about 18,000 residents. The town hall was firmly in the hands of the middle class. Merchants, senior officials, industrialists, lawyers, teachers and master craftsmen controlled the town's destiny.
